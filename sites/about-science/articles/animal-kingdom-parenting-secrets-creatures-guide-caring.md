Imagine thinking your sleepless nights with a newborn were exhausting—until you discover the burying beetle's parenting routine. These industrious insects transform corpses into perfectly preserved meals for their young, kneading dead animals with mouth and anal secretions into slick meat balls. The devoted parents bury these macabre delicacies and feed their offspring regurgitated carcass bits, helping baby beetles balloon to 200 times their birth size in just six days.

As science journalist Elizabeth Preston notes in her captivating new book *The Creatures' Guide to Caring*, "A newborn human growing at that rate would be the size of a beluga whale in less than a week." Suddenly, those 3 AM feeding sessions don't seem quite so overwhelming.

## When Biology Meets Parenting Reality

Preston's journey into animal parenting began with a deeply personal question that haunts countless new parents: "If so many people have done it before you, and are doing it right now—if so many animals are doing it without books or apps or advice to heed—why is it the hardest thing you've ever done?"

This existential puzzle, born from her own early parenting experiences, launched Preston into a fascinating exploration of how creatures across the animal kingdom have mastered the art of raising young. Her book transforms what could be a dry scientific survey into an entertaining and enlightening journey through nature's most extraordinary parenting strategies.

The narrative opens with a scene that perfectly captures the surreal nature of early parenthood. Preston finds herself caring for her infant in a dark room, feeling isolated from the world—until she imagines regurgitating food into her child's mouth like a wasp. "Sometimes I get mixed up because I still don't get enough sleep. What can I say? I have kids," she deadpans, setting the tone for a book that balances scientific rigor with parental humor.

## Evolutionary Parenting Across Species

One of the book's most compelling revelations involves fish fathers, Earth's pioneering parents. Despite their vast evolutionary distance from humans, male three-spined sticklebacks experience remarkably similar hormonal changes during childcare. As these aquatic fathers tend their eggs, genes producing oxytocin—the same bonding hormone crucial to human parent-child relationships—activate in their brains. They also produce progesterone and estrogen, hormones that influence their responses to threats and their caregiving behaviors.

These parallels suggest that the biological foundations of parenting run deeper than previously imagined, connecting species separated by hundreds of millions of years of evolution.

## Nature's Most Extreme Mothers

Perhaps no creatures exemplify fierce maternal dedication like spotted hyenas. Female hyenas endure what Preston diplomatically describes as "a lot of tearing" during birth, pushing cubs through pseudopenises in one of nature's most traumatic delivery processes. Yet these mothers form lifelong bonds with their offspring, aggressively shouldering aside other adults at kills to ensure their cubs get the choicest meat.

This protective instinct mirrors human parental behavior in surprisingly familiar ways. Just as hyena mothers fight for their cubs' access to food, human parents might cut to the front of a buffet line to secure the only dish their picky child will actually eat.

## The Dark Side of Animal Parenting

Preston doesn't shy away from parenting's more troubling aspects. Long-tailed skink mothers may devour their entire clutch when faced with repeated predator attacks, apparently calculating that starting over beats continuing a losing battle. Marmosets and tamarins, species that depend on community support for childcare, are more likely to reject babies when adequate help isn't available.

These harsh realities reflect similar challenges humans face when confronting unwanted pregnancies or insufficient resources for childcare. Preston suggests this difficulty exists partly because humans, like many cooperative species, evolved to parent within supportive communities rather than as isolated nuclear families.

## Finding Comfort in Evolutionary Solutions

The book's ultimate message offers profound reassurance to struggling parents everywhere. Across hundreds of millions of years, countless species have developed nearly infinite approaches to successfully raising young. From single-parent fish to community-supported primates, nature demonstrates that there's no single "correct" way to parent.

For readers navigating any stage of child-rearing—whether anticipating parenthood, dealing with demanding toddlers, or watching grown children leave home—Preston's exploration reveals a fundamental truth: we're not alone in finding parenting challenging. Even creatures with millions of years of evolutionary experience still struggle with the monumental task of raising the next generation.

*The Creatures' Guide to Caring* transforms our understanding of both animal behavior and human parenting, revealing the deep biological connections that unite all caring parents across the tree of life. In a world where parenting advice flows endlessly from experts and apps, Preston reminds us that sometimes the most valuable wisdom comes from observing the creatures who've been perfecting the art of childcare since long before humans walked the Earth.
