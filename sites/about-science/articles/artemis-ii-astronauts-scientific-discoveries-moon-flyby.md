# Artemis II Astronauts Deliver Scientific Treasure Trove Before Earth Return

As the Artemis II crew hurtles toward their scheduled April 10 splashdown, scientists on Earth are already immersed in analyzing an extraordinary collection of lunar observations. The four astronauts have achieved something remarkable: conducting real-time science during humanity's first moon flyby in over five decades.

![The boundary between lunar day and night is etched in stark relief, pebbled by jagged craters, in this shot of the eastern edge of the South Pole-Aitkin basin](https://www.sciencenews.org/wp-content/uploads/sites/2/2026/04/040826_LG_artemis_sciencefolo_main.jpg)

"We actually got to be scientists doing science," declares Marie Henderson, Artemis II deputy lunar science lead at NASA's Goddard Space Flight Center. The statement captures the electric atmosphere surrounding this historic mission's scientific achievements.

## A Choreographed Dance of Discovery

The crew—NASA astronauts Reid Wiseman, Christina Koch, Victor Glover, and Canadian Space Agency's Jeremy Hansen—executed a carefully planned seven-hour scientific observation marathon during their April 6 lunar flyby. Working in pairs, they positioned themselves at Orion's windows, capturing photographs and recordings while maintaining constant communication with mission control.

![Two women and one man sit at a desk looking at large screens with photos of the moon and a sign reading "Mission Control Center"](https://www.sciencenews.org/wp-content/uploads/sites/2/2026/04/040826_LG_artemis_sciencefolo_inline1.jpg)

Their extensive pre-mission geology training proved invaluable as they delivered vivid "sit reps" (situational reports) to Earth. The astronauts' poetic descriptions painted the lunar landscape in unprecedented detail: handprints etched in rock, pinpricks of light piercing a cosmic lampshade, islands floating in seas of darkness, and formations resembling dinosaur footprints.

"We trained them to describe it like they see it," explained Artemis lunar science lead Kelsey Young during an April 7 briefing. This approach has yielded thousands of photographs and audio recordings that continue streaming back to Earth, keeping scientific spirits soaring high.

## Witnessing Cosmic Fireworks

Perhaps the most thrilling discovery came from observing impact flashes—brief bursts of light created when micrometeorites strike the lunar surface. These phenomena had never been witnessed firsthand by human observers in space.

The opportunity arose during a spectacular 54-minute solar eclipse, as the crew oriented their spacecraft so the moon blocked the sun's brilliance. In this darkness, any impact flashes would become visible.

![A dark orb with a fuzzy white halo](https://www.sciencenews.org/wp-content/uploads/sites/2/2026/04/040826_LG_artemis_sciencefolo_inline4.jpg)

"I don't know if I expected to have the group see any on this mission," Young admitted. Yet the results exceeded all expectations.

Commander Wiseman described the moment: "The eclipse occurred, and then we had five minutes of human emotional reaction to staring at that orb floating in the vastness of space. Then right after that, somebody in the cabin said 'Let's look for impact flashes,' and immediately we saw one or two or three."

![Four astronauts wear solar eclipse glasses](https://www.sciencenews.org/wp-content/uploads/sites/2/2026/04/040826_LG_artemis_sciencefolo_inline5_b17360.jpg)

The crew reported at least four—possibly six—colorless pinpricks of light lasting mere milliseconds during a 30-minute observation period. Their detailed reports of timing and location are being cross-referenced with ground-based observations and NASA's Lunar Reconnaissance Orbiter data to potentially identify new craters.

"You probably saw the surprise and shock on my face," Young recalled. "And though I was not in the Science Evaluation Room, I heard there were audible screams of delight."

This data proves crucial for future lunar missions, as these same micrometeorites will pose risks to surface astronauts and their equipment.

## The Human Advantage: Seeing in Living Color

Human eyes possess a remarkable advantage over robotic explorers—the ability to detect subtle color variations that cameras cannot capture. This capability delivered immediate scientific rewards.

"I was eagerly awaiting if they saw any other colors than black, white, shades of gray," Young explained. "They did indeed, right away."

![Flat gray moon terrain is punctuated by bright craters surrounded by rays](https://www.sciencenews.org/wp-content/uploads/sites/2/2026/04/040826_LG_artemis_sciencefolo_inline6.jpg)

The astronauts identified green hues surrounding the bright Aristarchus crater, while other regions appeared distinctly brown. These color observations provide vital clues about lunar surface chemistry that could guide future exploration strategies.

Even more fascinating was how Earth's presence affected their lunar observations. Glover described the contrast as "the difference between an LED display and a painting—you can do things with light that you can't do with paint colors."

![The curved surface of the moon in the foreground looks like a rich brown, not its more familiar gray. A blue and white crescent of earth rises in the distance](https://www.sciencenews.org/wp-content/uploads/sites/2/2026/04/040826_LG_artemis_sciencefolo_inline7_97f5bf.jpg)

Koch noted a dramatic transformation: "The moon turned into a sponge of light. As soon as the Earth got close enough to be in my field of view to take them both in at the same time, [the moon] dulled, it turned into a sponge, it's almost like it went matte." The lunar surface shifted from "electric gray" to "olive brown."

![Two astronauts look up in a dark spacecraft. The only light is on their faces. Both wear glasses and hone is holding a camera](https://www.sciencenews.org/wp-content/uploads/sites/2/2026/04/040826_LG_artemis_sciencefolo_inline3.jpg)

Practical challenges emerged too. Koch requested help covering reflective spacecraft elements with a black t-shirt to prevent window glare, suggesting future missions should include darkroom cloth in their equipment manifest.

## Geological Revelations Through Impact Craters

Impact craters served as what Young called "a geologist's cheat code"—natural excavations revealing deep subsurface materials otherwise inaccessible. The crew's detailed observations of Ohm crater proved particularly valuable.

This impact site features bright rays of material extending outward from central peaks—evidence of rock liquefaction during impact, similar to water splashing upward in a pond. The astronauts noted color and brightness variations in these rays compared to surrounding darker terrain, suggesting excavated deep material overlaying older surface deposits.

Glover's observation of distinct layers within crater walls and color differences between floors, walls, and surrounding terrain provides crucial data for understanding lunar geological history and chronology.

The crew even proposed names for two previously unnamed craters: "Integrity" (their spacecraft's nickname) and "Carroll" (honoring Commander Wiseman's late wife). These names will be submitted to the International Astronomical Union upon their return.

![A dark orb floats in a fuzzy white halo. A darkened shape looms to the left](https://www.sciencenews.org/wp-content/uploads/sites/2/2026/04/040826_LG_artemis_sciencefolo_inline10.jpg)

## Looking Toward Future Horizons

During their eclipse observations, the astronauts spotted other planets aligned in the cosmic darkness. One astronaut noted seeing a distinctly red celestial body—likely Mars, as Young pointed out: "the future of where we're going."

These nuanced observations will directly inform future Artemis missions, helping scientists determine optimal landing sites to maximize scientific value. The crew's real-time geological assessments provide unprecedented ground truth for planning humanity's return to the lunar surface.

As thousands of photographs and hours of audio recordings continue transmitting to Earth, the scientific treasure trove from Artemis II promises to reshape our understanding of lunar geology, surface processes, and the ongoing bombardment that has sculpted our nearest cosmic neighbor for billions of years.

The mission demonstrates that while robotic explorers provide valuable data, human observers bring irreplaceable capabilities—instantaneous decision-making, adaptive observation strategies, and the ability to notice subtle details that could transform our cosmic perspective.
