![About 90 tons of plastic waste collected from Hawaiian waters](https://www.sciencenews.org/wp-content/uploads/sites/2/2026/03/033126_SN_pavement_main.jpg)

In the middle of the Pacific Ocean, Hawaii faces a unique environmental challenge that most places never encounter. The island chain sits directly in the path of the Great Pacific Garbage Patch, making it a collecting ground for marine debris, discarded fishing gear, and plastic waste that travels thousands of miles across the ocean. But Hawaiian researchers have developed an extraordinary solution: they're literally building roads with this ocean plastic.

This isn't just another recycling program. The Nets-to-Roads initiative represents the first-ever attempt to transform marine debris into functional infrastructure, tackling two massive problems simultaneously—plastic pollution and the need for durable road surfaces in Hawaii's challenging tropical environment.

## A Revolutionary Approach to Marine Waste

The project, led by chemist Jennifer Lynch at the Center for Marine Debris Research at Hawaii Pacific University, has already removed an impressive 90 metric tons of plastic trash from Pacific waters. More than a metric ton of fishing nets alone have been transformed into Hawaiian roadways, marking a dramatic shift in how we think about waste management.

"We're extremely concerned about the shedding of plastics or other chemicals into the environment," Lynch explains, noting the potential health risks from toxic plastic additives that can cause hormone disruption, chronic inflammation, and reproductive problems. This concern drives the rigorous testing protocols that make this project truly groundbreaking.

The process begins with marine biologist Mafalda de Freitas and her team collecting debris from beaches and ocean waters. They meticulously sort through the waste, specifically targeting materials made from polyethylene—a durable plastic found in milk jugs, yogurt containers, and fishing nets.

![Road crew working on experimental pavement sections in Hawaii](https://www.sciencenews.org/wp-content/uploads/sites/2/2026/03/033126_SN_pavement_inline.jpg)

## From Ocean Floor to Road Surface

The transformation process is as fascinating as it is complex. The collected waste travels to the U.S. mainland where it undergoes shredding and grinding before returning to an Oahu-based pavement facility. There, it's mixed with traditional asphalt ingredients and a rubber additive called styrene-butadiene-styrene, which enhances durability and flexibility.

The first experimental phase in 2022 created three distinct test strips along Ewa Beach on Oahu's southwestern shore. One section used traditional asphalt with the rubber additive, another incorporated ground marine waste with rubber, and a third combined the waste with asphalt but no rubber component.

## Critical Safety Testing Reveals Promising Results

The most crucial question surrounding this innovation concerns microplastic leaching—whether road wear might release harmful particles into the environment. After eleven months of exposure to Hawaii's intense weather conditions, researchers conducted extensive testing to simulate real-world scenarios.

Jeremy Axworthy, the marine biologist and lab manager who presented findings at the American Chemical Society meeting in Atlanta, describes their comprehensive approach: "We want to empirically test for [leaching] before this would ever be scaled up."

The team simulated stormwater runoff by applying filtered and sanitized water to road surfaces, then testing for individual plastic polymers. They also collected gravel dust through road sweeping to analyze for polymer content. The preliminary results offer significant hope—microplastic release from the experimental sections showed no significant difference compared to traditional asphalt roads.

## Expanding the Experiment

Encouraged by initial results, researchers launched a second phase in 2024 with five experimental pavement strips. This expanded study includes separate tests for fishing nets versus consumer plastic waste, each with and without rubber additives, plus a traditional control section.

Currently, the team employs industrial solvents to extract plastic polymers from road dust, enabling more precise quantification of any microplastic release. These advanced results will provide even clearer evidence about the safety and viability of plastic road surfaces.

## Unique Challenges of Island Infrastructure

Bill Buttlar, director of the Mizzou Asphalt Pavement and Innovation Lab at the University of Missouri, praises the program while highlighting Hawaii's distinctive infrastructure challenges. The tropical climate brings heavy rainfall and volcanic activity, creating underground instability that can cause road cracking.

"The main challenge to scaling this is getting the recipe right with the asphalt because what works in Hawaii may be a little different than what works in the Midwest," Buttlar notes.

## A Model for Global Innovation

While plastic paving initiatives exist in states like Missouri and Texas, Hawaii's marine debris approach represents uncharted territory. The project addresses the islands' unique position as an unwilling collector of ocean plastic while creating valuable infrastructure from waste materials.

This innovative solution demonstrates how environmental challenges can drive technological breakthroughs. As researchers continue refining their formulations and conducting safety assessments, Hawaii's plastic roads may become a model for coastal communities worldwide facing similar marine debris challenges.

The success of this program could revolutionize how we approach both plastic pollution and infrastructure development, transforming one of our greatest environmental problems into a sustainable resource for building the future.
