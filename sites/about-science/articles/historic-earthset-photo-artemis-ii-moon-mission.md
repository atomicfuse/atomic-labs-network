In a breathtaking moment that will be remembered alongside the most iconic space photographs in history, Artemis II astronauts have captured the first-ever image of Earth setting behind the moon. This extraordinary photograph, taken on April 6, 2026, at 6:41 p.m. Eastern time, marks another remarkable milestone for NASA's ambitious lunar mission.

![An Artemis II astronaut captured the crescent Earth setting behind the moon at 6:41 p.m. Eastern time on April 6, 2026, as the Orion spacecraft flew around the lunar farside.](https://www.sciencenews.org/wp-content/uploads/sites/2/2026/04/040726_LG_artemis_earthset_main.jpg)

The stunning image serves as a powerful counterpart to the legendary 1968 "Earthrise" photograph captured by Apollo 8 astronaut William Anders. That spontaneous shot of Earth appearing over the moon's horizon became one of the most influential photographs ever taken, inspiring the environmental movement and fundamentally changing how humanity viewed our planet's place in the cosmos.

## A Carefully Orchestrated Moment

Unlike the serendipitous nature of the original Earthrise photo, this "Earthset" image was meticulously planned by the mission team. The photograph reveals Earth as a delicate crescent, with swirling clouds visible over Australia and Oceania illuminated by the sun's rays.

![The curve of the moon is on the left side of this image with a fingernail crescent of Earth seen at right.](https://www.sciencenews.org/wp-content/uploads/sites/2/2026/04/040726_LG_artemis_earthset_inline-1.jpg?w=575)

The lunar surface dominates the foreground with its characteristic brownish tint, a feature that particularly fascinated the astronauts during their seven-hour flyby of the moon. "The more I look at the moon, the browner and browner it looks," one crew member observed, highlighting the unexpected colors they witnessed firsthand.

## A Sequence of Dramatic Views

The mission captured a remarkable sequence showing Earth's gradual disappearance behind the lunar horizon. Beginning 36 minutes before the actual Earthset, the crew documented the celestial dance between our home planet and its natural satellite.

![The cratered curve of the moon is in the foreground wiht a blueish crescent of Earth in the background.](https://www.sciencenews.org/wp-content/uploads/sites/2/2026/04/040726_LG_artemis_earthset_inline-2.jpg?w=575)

Six minutes before Earth disappeared completely from view, the astronauts captured another striking image showing the two celestial bodies in close proximity, emphasizing the dramatic scale and beauty of this cosmic ballet.

![A tiny fingernail sliver of Earth appears above the curvature of the moon.](https://www.sciencenews.org/wp-content/uploads/sites/2/2026/04/040726_LG_artemis_earthset_inline-3.jpg?w=575)

As Earth finally set behind the moon, the lunar craters became etched in stark relief against the darkness of space. The Artemis II astronauts achieved a historic first—becoming the first humans to witness these dramatic features on the moon's farside with their own eyes since the Apollo era.

## A Moment of Reflection

The timing of this photograph held special significance, as it was captured just before the spacecraft temporarily lost contact with Earth during its journey around the moon's farside. This brief communication blackout added an emotional weight to the moment, as the astronauts prepared to disappear behind the lunar horizon.

Astronaut Christina Koch reflected on the profound experience with words that captured the crew's deep connection to home: "Ultimately, we will always choose Earth. We will always choose each other."

Approximately one hour after capturing this historic image, the Artemis II crew emerged from behind the moon to begin their journey back to Earth, carrying with them not just scientific data and samples, but also this powerful reminder of our planet's beauty and fragility as seen from the depths of space.

This "Earthset" photograph stands as a testament to human exploration and our enduring bond with the pale blue dot we call home, continuing the tradition of space photography that helps us see ourselves and our world from an entirely new perspective.
