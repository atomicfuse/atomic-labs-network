# Revolutionary Study Reveals How Mental Imagery Works in the Human Brain

![The ability to create mental pictures helps us to remember, plan, navigate and create works of art.](https://www.sciencenews.org/wp-content/uploads/sites/2/2026/04/040926_DK_imagery_main.jpg)

The remarkable ability to visualize objects in our mind's eye—from recalling a loved one's face to imagining a future vacation—has long fascinated scientists and philosophers alike. This extraordinary cognitive power enables us to remember past experiences, envision future possibilities, navigate complex environments, and create magnificent works of art. Now, groundbreaking research published in *Science* has finally revealed the neural mechanisms behind this mysterious phenomenon.

## Breakthrough Discovery at the Cellular Level

In a stunning revelation that has electrified the neuroscience community, researchers have demonstrated that the very same brain cells that fire when we see an object also spring into action when we simply imagine it. This discovery represents the first direct evidence at the individual neuron level of how mental imagery operates in the human brain.

"This was a study that the field was waiting for," declares Nadine Dijkstra, a neuroscientist at University College London who wasn't involved in the research. The implications of this finding extend far beyond basic neuroscience, potentially revolutionizing our understanding of memory, creativity, and even mental health disorders.

## Unprecedented Look Inside the Living Brain

Led by neuroscientist Ueli Rutishauser at Cedars-Sinai Medical Center in Los Angeles, the research team faced an extraordinary challenge: how to observe individual neurons in action within living human brains. Their solution was both ingenious and ethically sound—they collaborated with 16 adults with epilepsy who already had electrodes implanted in their brains for medical treatment to locate seizure origins.

This rare opportunity allowed the scientists to record activity from over 700 neurons in the ventral temporal cortex, a critical brain region responsible for processing visual objects. The precision of their measurements was unprecedented, capturing the electrical whispers of individual brain cells as participants viewed hundreds of carefully selected images.

## The Experiment That Changed Everything

The research unfolded in two dramatic phases. First, participants viewed hundreds of images spanning five distinct categories: human faces, written text, plants, animals, and everyday objects. During this phase, approximately 450 neurons demonstrated selective responses to specific categories, with machine learning analysis revealing that 80 percent of these neurons were attuned to particular visual features within the images.

Then came the moment of truth. Six participants were asked to close their eyes and vividly imagine some of the objects they had previously seen. The results were nothing short of extraordinary: about 40 percent of the neurons that had fired during actual perception also activated when the same objects were visualized purely in the mind's eye.

To validate their findings, the researchers employed an almost science-fiction-like approach—they used the neural recordings to reconstruct the mental images participants were attempting to recall, essentially reading minds through brain activity patterns.

## Revolutionary Implications for Brain Science

"These observations support the idea that the human brain implements what some call a generative model," explains study co-author Varun Wadia, a neuroscientist at Cedars-Sinai. "The reactivation of the neural code used to perceive objects enables us to create mental images."

This discovery validates a fundamental hypothesis that has guided neuroscience research for decades: that perception and imagination share common neural pathways. However, until this study, scientists could only observe this overlap at the level of brain regions, not individual neurons.

## Hope for Mental Health Breakthroughs

The implications extend far beyond basic science into the realm of mental health treatment. Disruptions to mental imagery have been linked to several psychiatric conditions, including schizophrenia and post-traumatic stress disorder (PTSD). Understanding the precise neuronal mechanisms underlying mental imagery could pave the way for revolutionary therapeutic approaches.

"Better understanding the neuronal underpinning of mental imagery could pave the way for new therapies," Wadia emphasizes, suggesting that this research might eventually lead to targeted treatments for conditions where mental imagery is impaired.

## The Future of Imagination Research

While this study represents a monumental breakthrough, it also opens new frontiers for exploration. Rutishauser acknowledges that whether similar mechanisms operate in more complex forms of mental imagery—such as the ability to envision entirely novel artistic creations—remains an open question.

"But this presents a clear hypothesis for how things like this could work," he notes, suggesting that this research provides a foundation for understanding even the most creative aspects of human imagination.

## A Window Into Human Consciousness

This groundbreaking research represents more than just another scientific discovery—it offers a profound glimpse into the very nature of human consciousness and creativity. By revealing that the boundary between perception and imagination is far more fluid than previously thought, these findings challenge our understanding of how the brain constructs our reality.

As we stand on the threshold of new discoveries about the mind's most mysterious capabilities, this research illuminates the extraordinary biological machinery that makes us uniquely human—our ability to see not just with our eyes, but with our minds.
