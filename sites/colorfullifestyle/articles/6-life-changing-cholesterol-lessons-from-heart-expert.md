When it comes to understanding cholesterol and heart disease, few people have the expertise of Prof Børge Nordestgaard. This distinguished clinician and scientist from the University of Copenhagen has spent four decades battling cholesterol in both laboratory research and clinical practice. His groundbreaking work earned him the prestigious 2020 Marie and August Krogh Prize, and his insights could literally save your life.

"I've been fighting cholesterol for 40 years in the lab and in the clinic," Prof Nordestgaard reflects. "As a young doctor in Denmark and the UK, I witnessed people dying from cholesterol problems and heart disease constantly. While we've made progress in some regions, the global challenge persists."

Despite improvements in cholesterol awareness across Europe and the US, heart disease remains the world's leading killer. The problem is now spreading to developing countries as Western dietary habits take hold. This makes Prof Nordestgaard's expertise more crucial than ever.

Here are his six essential lessons for managing cholesterol and protecting your heart health:

## 1. Forget Everything You Know About 'Good' and 'Bad' Cholesterol

Let's start with a reality check: cholesterol isn't inherently good or bad – it's simply a natural fat that gives egg yolks their golden color. Your body uses cholesterol to build cell membranes and create essential hormones like estrogen and testosterone. Your brain contains significant amounts of cholesterol too.

Here's the key insight: your liver already produces all the cholesterol your body needs. The problem isn't cholesterol itself, but what happens when it gets stuck in your artery walls. Since your body lacks enzymes to break down cholesterol, it accumulates and causes atherosclerosis – the narrowing of arteries that leads to heart attacks and strokes.

While LDL (low-density lipoprotein) cholesterol is genuinely harmful, Prof Nordestgaard challenges the popular notion of HDL as "good" cholesterol. "The concept that HDL cholesterol is 'good' is a misinterpretation of the data," he explains. "HDL is neutral – it doesn't harm you, but it doesn't really help either."

The real takeaway? Focus on reducing all cholesterol types except HDL, rather than trying to boost your "good" cholesterol.

## 2. The Hidden Cholesterol Villain You've Never Heard Of

Until recently, medical focus centered on HDL and LDL cholesterol. But Prof Nordestgaard's research has identified a potentially more dangerous player: remnant cholesterol.

"I call this 'remnant cholesterol' because it's what remains after you remove HDL and LDL," he explains. "This remnant cholesterol could be even more harmful for cardiovascular disease."

Remnant cholesterol particles are larger than LDL and contain higher cholesterol concentrations. They also carry triglycerides, another blood fat that increases heart disease risk. The concerning reality? If you're overweight or obese, you likely have elevated remnant cholesterol levels.

The good news is that researchers are developing targeted treatments. Recent studies show six different medications can reduce remnant cholesterol by at least half, including statins already in widespread use.

## 3. Cholesterol Doesn't Just Correlate with Heart Disease – It Causes It

![Red Human Heart on green background. 3d render](https://c02.purpledshub.com/uploads/sites/41/2026/03/6-ways-manage-cholesterol-heart.jpg?webp=1&w=1200)

The relationship between cholesterol and heart disease isn't simply associative – it's causative. This understanding dates back over a century to Russian scientist Prof Nikolai Anichkov, who demonstrated that feeding cholesterol to rabbits caused atherosclerosis.

Here's how the process unfolds: cholesterol particles (LDL and remnants) are too large to penetrate artery walls smoothly, so they become trapped. This triggers inflammation, similar to how your body responds to a splinter. Immune cells called macrophages attempt to consume the cholesterol but can't digest it properly, leading to their death and the formation of atherosclerotic plaques.

These cholesterol-rich plaques can rupture, causing blood clots that block blood flow to the heart or brain, resulting in heart attacks or strokes.

Understanding your target cholesterol levels is crucial:
- If you're healthy: Keep LDL below 3.0 mmol/L (115 mg/dL)
- If you have heart disease, stroke, or severe diabetes: Aim for LDL below 1.4 mmol/L (55 mg/dL)

## 4. Eat Like You're Saving the Planet

![Anti-inflammatory vegetables, fruits, legumes, nuts and spices relieving health problems - vegetables, fruits, legumes, nuts and spices for health benefits on wooden table](https://c02.purpledshub.com/uploads/sites/41/2026/03/6-ways-manage-cholesterol-plant-diet.jpg?webp=1&w=1200)

Reducing cholesterol through diet isn't complicated, but it requires mindful eating. The key is minimizing saturated and trans fats, which impair your liver's ability to clear cholesterol from your bloodstream.

Prof Nordestgaard offers a simple rule: "If it's from a plant, it won't contain saturated fat, so it's generally safe to eat. If it's from an animal, investigate further."

There are exceptions – coconut oil and palm oil contain high levels of saturated fats and contribute to deforestation. However, you can enjoy unlimited whole plant foods guilt-free.

"The best advice I can give for lowering cholesterol is to eat the way you would to prevent global warming," Prof Nordestgaard suggests. Plant-rich diets benefit both your arteries and the environment.

This doesn't mean eliminating animal products entirely. If you enjoy meat and fish, consider South Asian-style preparation – small strips combined with noodles or rice and plenty of vegetables.

While exercise doesn't directly impact cholesterol levels, it does reduce triglycerides and can help maintain a healthy weight, which lowers remnant cholesterol. Prof Nordestgaard personally walks whenever possible and cycles to work daily while maintaining a plant-rich diet that includes fish.

## 5. Don't Fear Statins – Embrace Their Life-Saving Power

Statins represent one of modern medicine's greatest success stories for adult disease prevention. These medications effectively lower LDL cholesterol, reduce heart attacks and strokes, and ultimately help people live longer.

"Nothing we can do to prevent diseases in adulthood works as well as statins," Prof Nordestgaard states confidently. He's been taking the maximum dosage since turning 50 and plans to continue for life.

Despite media-fueled concerns about side effects, statins are remarkably safe. Prof Nordestgaard notes that statins have fewer side effects than any other drug he's studied in his extensive career. The negative publicity stems from commercial interests rather than scientific evidence.

The actual risks are minimal:
- Only 15 in 10,000 people experience muscle aches or fatigue
- Serious complications affect perhaps 1 in 100,000 people
- Severe side effects are extremely rare

"These headlines were written for commercial reasons and are nothing but fake news," he emphasizes. "Before statins, we constantly discussed side effects with patients. After statins became available, those conversations largely disappeared."

The recommended starting age is 50 for men and 55 for women.

## 6. Your Genes Might Be Working Against You

Diet isn't the only cholesterol culprit. Genetic factors can significantly elevate cholesterol levels, leading to early heart attacks and strokes regardless of lifestyle choices.

Two key genetic conditions affect cholesterol:

**Familial Hypercholesterolemia (FH):** Affects 1 in 250 people due to genetic mutations that reduce the liver's ability to remove cholesterol from blood.

**High Lipoprotein(a) or Lp(a):** Affects 1 in 5 people and represents a fourth type of cholesterol-containing particle. While its evolutionary purpose may involve blood clotting during childbirth, it can create larger, more dangerous clots in people with atherosclerosis.

The encouraging news is that screening and treatment options are expanding rapidly. The European Union's Safe Hearts Plan, published in December 2025, recommends early screening for both conditions. Several countries, including Slovenia, the Netherlands, and Norway, have implemented successful screening programs.

For Lp(a), the future looks particularly promising. Five randomized trials are currently testing Lp(a)-targeting drugs, and gene-silencing medications can reduce Lp(a) levels by up to 98%.

## Taking Action for Your Heart Health

Prof Nordestgaard's decades of research deliver a clear message: heart disease remains our biggest killer, but it's largely preventable. By understanding cholesterol's true nature, adopting a plant-rich diet, considering statins when appropriate, and screening for genetic risk factors, you can significantly reduce your cardiovascular disease risk.

"As long as heart disease remains our biggest killer, there will always be more work to do," Prof Nordestgaard concludes. "We must keep focusing on what's causing it – cholesterol."

The path to heart health isn't complex, but it requires commitment to evidence-based strategies. Your future self will thank you for taking action today.
