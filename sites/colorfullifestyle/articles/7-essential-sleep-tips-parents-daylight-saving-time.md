When clocks "spring forward" at 1am on the last Sunday of March (March 29th this year), families everywhere brace for the inevitable disruption to their carefully crafted sleep routines. That lost hour doesn't just disappear quietly – it often leaves everyone feeling groggy, out of sync, and struggling to adjust.

For parents already juggling sleepless nights and early morning wake-ups, the time change can feel like an extra challenge you didn't sign up for. The good news? With some thoughtful preparation, you can help your entire family transition more smoothly into daylight saving time.

## Why Sleep Preparation Matters

Your body's internal clock – and your child's – needs time to adjust to schedule changes. Rather than waiting for the shock of losing an hour overnight, taking proactive steps in the days leading up to the change can make all the difference.

Experts from Northern Lights and baby sleep coach Jo Rammell from The Little Sleep Spot emphasize that small adjustments to your environment and routines can significantly ease this transition.

## The Power of Proper Lighting

One of the most overlooked factors in quality sleep is lighting. Your body relies on light cues to understand when it's time to be alert versus when it's time to wind down.

"Many people overlook how lighting affects the body's internal clock," explains a spokesperson for Northern Lights. "Exposure to bright or cool-toned lighting late in the evening can make it harder for the body to recognise that it's time to rest."

Creating a softer, dimmer environment as bedtime approaches helps signal to both you and your children that the day is ending. Consider dimming bright lights in living areas and bedrooms at least an hour before sleep time.

## 7 Expert Tips for Parents

Baby sleep coach Jo Rammell from The Little Sleep Spot shares her proven strategies for helping families navigate the time change:

### 1. Gradually Shift Bedtime by 15 Minutes

If bedtime battles are already part of your nightly routine, start preparing several days ahead. Move bedtime earlier by 15 minutes each night leading up to the clock change.

For example, if your child typically goes to bed at 8 pm, try 7:45 pm the first night, then 7:30 pm the next, and so on. By the time clocks spring forward, your child will already be adjusted to sleeping an hour earlier. After the time change, you can return to the normal 8 pm bedtime instead of dealing with what would feel like a 9 pm schedule.

Remember to adjust daytime naps and meal times by the same 15-minute increments to keep your child's entire schedule in sync.

### 2. Be Patient with the Adjustment Period

Every child responds differently to schedule changes. While some adapt quickly, others may need up to two weeks to fully adjust to the new timing. Most families find that about a week is typical for settling into the new routine.

Don't get discouraged if the first few nights feel challenging – this is completely normal.

### 3. Maximize Daylight Exposure

Natural light plays a crucial role in regulating your family's internal clocks. Make sure your children get plenty of outdoor playtime during daylight hours, especially during the transition period.

This natural light exposure helps reset their circadian rhythms and can speed up the adjustment process significantly.

### 4. Invest in Quality Blackout Blinds

As spring progresses and evenings stay lighter longer, darkness becomes your ally in promoting healthy sleep. Blackout blinds help trigger the release of melatonin, your body's natural sleep hormone.

Any parent who has tried convincing a toddler that it's bedtime while the sun is still shining knows this investment is worth its weight in gold!

### 5. Choose the Right Nighttime Lighting

If you need lighting in your child's room for nighttime feedings or comfort, the color matters more than you might think. Blue and white electronic lights can interfere with sleep by suppressing melatonin production.

Instead, opt for red or amber-based nightlights, which are much less likely to disrupt your child's natural sleep hormones.

### 6. Create a Screen-Free Wind-Down Period

Avoid screens for 30 minutes to one hour before bedtime. The blue light emitted by tablets, phones, and televisions can significantly impact melatonin production and overall sleep quality.

Replace screen time with calming activities like reading together, playing quiet games, or singing lullabies. Combine this with warm, dim lighting to create the perfect pre-sleep environment.

### 7. When to Do Nothing at All

Here's a refreshing perspective: if your family doesn't have a set routine yet, or if you're already dealing with very early wake-ups, you might choose to do absolutely nothing special for the time change.

Since you're losing an hour of sleep overnight, that persistent early bird in your house might actually wake up at a more reasonable time after the clocks spring forward!

## Making the Transition Easier for Everyone

Remember that this adjustment period affects adults too. While you're helping your children adapt, don't forget to apply these same principles to your own sleep routine. Gradually shifting your bedtime, maximizing daylight exposure, and creating a calming evening environment will help you feel more rested and better equipped to handle any bumps in your children's sleep schedules.

The key to successfully navigating daylight saving time lies in preparation and patience. By making small, consistent changes in the days leading up to the clock change, you're setting your entire family up for a smoother transition into longer, brighter days ahead.

With these expert-backed strategies, you can turn what's often a challenging time into an opportunity to strengthen healthy sleep habits that will benefit your family long after the clocks have changed.
