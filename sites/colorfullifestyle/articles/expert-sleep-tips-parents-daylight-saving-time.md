When daylight saving time arrives on the last Sunday in March, it brings more than just longer evenings – it often brings sleep disruption for the whole family. That precious hour we lose can leave everyone feeling groggy and off-balance, especially parents who are already juggling sleep challenges with little ones.

![Featured Image](https://www.rte.ie/images/00241d21-1600.jpg)

The good news? With some thoughtful preparation and expert guidance, you can minimize the impact of this twice-yearly transition. Sleep experts and baby sleep coaches share their top strategies for making the clock change less stressful for everyone.

## Why Lighting Matters More Than You Think

Before diving into specific tips, it's important to understand how much lighting affects our internal clocks. Exposure to bright or cool-toned lights in the evening can confuse our bodies about when it's time to rest. Creating a softer, dimmer environment as bedtime approaches helps signal that the day is winding down.

"Many people overlook how lighting affects the body's internal clock," explain experts from Northern Lights. "Dimming bright lights in your living room or bedroom as bedtime approaches can help create a calmer environment and support a more relaxed transition into sleep."

## 7 Expert Tips for Managing the Clock Change

Baby sleep coach Jo Rammell from The Little Sleep Spot shares these practical strategies for parents:

### 1. Gradually Shift Bedtime by 15 Minutes

If bedtimes are already a challenge in your household, start preparing a few days early. Move bedtime 15 minutes earlier each night leading up to the change. For example, if your child typically goes to bed at 8 PM, try 7:45 PM, then 7:30 PM, and so on.

This gradual approach means that when the clocks spring forward, your child will naturally return to their regular 8 PM bedtime instead of suddenly shifting to 9 PM. Remember to adjust nap times and meals by the same 15-minute increments.

### 2. Set Realistic Expectations

Don't expect instant adaptation – that's setting yourself up for frustration. Every child responds differently to schedule changes, and it typically takes anywhere from a few days to two weeks for full adjustment. Most families find their groove again within about a week.

### 3. Maximize Daylight Exposure

Natural light is one of our most powerful tools for regulating body clocks. Encourage plenty of outdoor playtime during daylight hours, especially during the transition period. This exposure helps children (and adults) adjust their internal clocks more quickly.

![Close up on peaceful face of Black baby girl sleeping in crib while mothers hand gently caressing kids hair in childrens bedroom](https://www.rte.ie/images/00241d24-614.jpg?ratio=1.78)

### 4. Invest in Quality Blackout Blinds

As evenings become lighter, darkness becomes even more crucial for sleep. Blackout blinds help promote melatonin production – our body's natural sleep hormone. Plus, convincing a toddler it's bedtime when the sun is still shining can be nearly impossible without proper room darkening.

### 5. Choose Sleep-Friendly Lighting

If you need lighting in your child's room, color matters. Blue and white lights can interfere with sleep, while red or amber-based lights are much gentler on our natural sleep cycles. These warmer tones don't suppress melatonin production as much as cooler lights do.

![Baby sleeping peacefully](https://www.rte.ie/images/00241d23-614.jpg?ratio=1.78)

### 6. Create a Screen-Free Wind-Down Period

Banish screens for 30 minutes to an hour before bedtime. The blue light from devices can seriously disrupt melatonin production and sleep quality. Instead, focus on calming activities like reading together, gentle games, or singing lullabies. This is also the perfect time to switch to warm, dim lighting throughout your home.

### 7. When in Doubt, Do Nothing

Here's a refreshing perspective: if your little one doesn't have an established routine yet, or if they're already waking up very early, consider riding out the change without major adjustments. Sometimes that "lost" hour can actually help normalize an overly early wake-up time.

## Making the Transition Smoother for Everyone

Remember, these sleep disruptions are temporary. Your family's natural rhythms will adjust, especially with consistent routines and patience. The key is being proactive rather than reactive – small changes made in advance can prevent bigger sleep battles later.

Whether you're dealing with a restless baby or trying to maintain your own sleep health as a busy parent, these evidence-based strategies can help your whole household navigate daylight saving time more smoothly. Sweet dreams!
