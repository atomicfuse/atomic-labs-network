When daylight saving time arrives and clocks spring forward at 1am on the last Sunday of March (March 29 this year), families everywhere face the challenge of losing that precious hour of sleep. For parents already juggling sleepless nights, early morning wake-ups, and mounting sleep debt, this transition can feel especially overwhelming.

![Featured image showing clocks changing](https://www.rte.ie/images/00241d21-1600.jpg)

The good news? You don't have to let the time change derail your family's sleep routine. With some thoughtful preparation and expert guidance, you can minimize the disruption and help everyone adjust more smoothly.

## Why Daylight Saving Time Hits Parents Harder

Losing an hour of sleep affects everyone, but parents face a double challenge. Not only do you need to adjust your own disrupted body clock, but you also have to help your little ones navigate the change. This can throw off carefully established routines and leave the whole family feeling tired and out of sorts.

![Mother holding crying newborn baby. Little baby is fussy, colic baby. Stressful frustrating moment for new mom.](https://www.rte.ie/images/00241d22-614.jpg?ratio=1.78)

Experts from Northern Lights and baby sleep coach Jo Rammell from The Little Sleep Spot emphasize that small, strategic changes to your environment and routines can make this transition much more manageable.

## The Power of Lighting in Sleep Success

Before diving into specific strategies, it's crucial to understand how lighting impacts your family's sleep. Many parents underestimate the role that light plays in regulating our internal body clocks.

As Northern Lights experts explain: "Exposure to bright or cool-toned lighting late in the evening can make it harder for the body to recognize that it's time to rest. Creating a softer, lower-lit environment helps signal that the day is ending and supports a more relaxed transition into sleep."

This principle applies to both adults and children, making it a cornerstone of successful sleep transitions.

## 7 Expert-Approved Sleep Strategies for Parents

### 1. Gradually Shift Bedtime by 15 Minutes

If bedtime battles are already part of your evening routine, start preparing several days before the clock change. Gradually move your child's bedtime earlier by 15 minutes each night.

For example, if your little one typically goes to bed at 8 PM, try this schedule:
- Day 1: 7:45 PM
- Day 2: 7:30 PM
- Day 3: 7:15 PM
- Day 4: 7:00 PM

By the time clocks spring forward, your child will already be adjusted to sleeping an hour earlier. After the change, you can return to the normal 8 PM bedtime instead of dealing with a sudden 9 PM shift.

Remember to adjust daytime naps and meal times by the same 15-minute increments to keep everything in sync.

### 2. Set Realistic Expectations for Adjustment

Don't expect your baby or toddler to instantly adapt to the time change. Every child responds differently, and the adjustment period can range from a few days to two weeks. Most families find that it takes about a week for everyone to feel back to normal.

![Close up on peaceful face of Black baby girl sleeping in crib while mothers hand gently caressing kids hair in childrens bedroom](https://www.rte.ie/images/00241d24-614.jpg?ratio=1.78)

Being patient with the process helps reduce stress for both you and your children during this transition period.

### 3. Maximize Natural Light Exposure

Light serves as one of the most powerful tools for regulating our body clocks. Make sure your children get plenty of outdoor playtime in natural daylight, especially during the week of the clock change.

This exposure helps their internal clocks adjust more quickly to the new schedule. Even a morning walk or afternoon play session in the backyard can make a significant difference.

### 4. Invest in Quality Blackout Blinds

As evenings become lighter with daylight saving time, darkness becomes even more valuable for promoting quality sleep. Blackout blinds help create the dark environment needed for melatonin production (our natural sleep hormone).

Plus, any parent who has tried convincing a toddler that it's bedtime while the sun is still shining knows this investment is worth every penny!

### 5. Choose the Right Color for Night Lighting

If you need lighting in your child's room for nighttime feedings or comfort, the color matters more than you might think. Blue and white lights can interfere with sleep by suppressing melatonin production.

Instead, opt for red or amber-based lighting, which has minimal impact on your child's natural sleep hormones.

![Baby sleeping peacefully](https://www.rte.ie/images/00241d23-614.jpg?ratio=1.78)

### 6. Create a Screen-Free Wind-Down Period

Avoid screens for 30 minutes to one hour before bedtime. The blue light emitted by devices can disrupt melatonin production and affect sleep quality.

Replace screen time with calming activities like:
- Reading favorite books together
- Playing quiet games
- Singing lullabies or gentle songs
- Cuddling and chatting about the day

Combine this screen-free time with warm, dim lighting to signal that sleep time is approaching.

### 7. Consider Doing Nothing (Really!)

If your little one doesn't have an established routine yet, or if they're already early risers, sometimes the best strategy is no strategy at all.

Since we lose an hour when clocks spring forward, that naturally early wake-up time might actually feel more manageable after the change. This hands-off approach can work particularly well for families still establishing their routines.

## Creating Your Family Sleep Success Plan

The key to navigating daylight saving time successfully lies in understanding that every family is different. Some children adapt quickly with minimal intervention, while others need more structured support.

Start by assessing your current sleep situation:
- Are bedtimes already challenging?
- Does your child wake very early?
- How established are your current routines?

Use these insights to choose which strategies will work best for your family. Remember, the goal isn't perfection – it's progress toward better sleep for everyone.

## The Long-Term Benefits

While adjusting to daylight saving time requires some effort upfront, successfully navigating this transition sets your family up for better sleep habits year-round. The strategies you implement now – from optimizing lighting to creating consistent routines – will continue benefiting your family's sleep quality long after the clocks have changed.

By taking a proactive approach and using these expert-backed techniques, you can transform what's often a stressful time into an opportunity to strengthen your family's sleep foundation. Sweet dreams await on the other side of this transition!
