# Anthropic's Claude Mythos Is Too Dangerous to Release — Here's Why That Should Terrify You

When an AI company says their latest model is too dangerous for public release, you should probably pay attention. That's exactly what happened this week when Anthropic dropped a bombshell announcement about their new Claude Mythos Preview model — an AI so skilled at finding security vulnerabilities that they're treating it like digital plutonium.

![Anthropic's Claude Mythos Preview model representation](https://static01.nyt.com/images/2026/04/06/business/ROOSE-claude/ROOSE-claude-videoSixteenByNine3000.jpg)

Instead of a typical product launch, Anthropic is taking an unprecedented approach: they're handing Mythos over to a exclusive club of 40+ tech giants — including Apple, Amazon, Microsoft, and even competitors like Google — through something called Project Glasswing. The mission? Use this AI's scary-good vulnerability detection skills to patch critical software before the bad guys figure out how to exploit it.

## The $100 Million Security Race

Anthropic isn't just making bold claims — they're putting serious money behind this initiative. The company is committing up to $100 million in Claude usage credits to help these organizations scan and secure both open-source and proprietary software.

"The goal is both to raise awareness and to give good actors a head start on the process of securing open-source and private infrastructure and code," explains Jared Kaplan, Anthropic's chief science officer.

Project Glasswing reads like a who's who of tech infrastructure: hardware providers like Cisco and Broadcom, open-source maintainers including the Linux Foundation, and pretty much every major cloud provider you can think of.

## Why This Is Actually Terrifying

Here's the thing that should keep you up at night: if Anthropic built an AI this good at finding security holes, what happens when someone else builds something similar but doesn't have the same ethical guardrails?

Logan Graham, who leads Anthropic's red team testing for dangerous AI capabilities, isn't mincing words. He calls Mythos "the starting point for what we think will be an industry change point, or reckoning, with what needs to happen now."

![Kevin Roose, technology reporter](https://static01.nyt.com/images/2018/02/20/multimedia/author-kevin-roose/author-kevin-roose-thumbLarge-v2.png)

Translation: we're entering an era where AI can systematically tear apart software security faster than humans can patch it. The only question is whether the good guys or bad guys get there first.

## The Pentagon Problem

This announcement comes on the heels of Anthropic's recent clash with the Pentagon over military AI applications. The company has been walking a tightrope between advancing AI capabilities and preventing their misuse — a balancing act that's getting more precarious as their models become more powerful.

The fact that they're proactively restricting Mythos access suggests they've learned something that genuinely spooked them during internal testing.

## What This Means for Your Digital Life

While you won't be able to download Claude Mythos Preview anytime soon, its existence has massive implications for cybersecurity. If this AI can rapidly identify vulnerabilities in critical infrastructure, we might see:

- Faster patching cycles for popular software
- More robust security in open-source projects that typically lack resources
- A potential arms race between defensive and offensive AI capabilities
- Possibly fewer zero-day exploits making it to market

## The Double-Edged Sword

Anthropic's approach is both reassuring and terrifying. It's reassuring because they're being responsible with genuinely dangerous technology. It's terrifying because it confirms that AI capabilities are advancing so rapidly that even the companies building these systems are scared of what they've created.

The real test will be whether this consortium model actually works — and whether other AI labs follow Anthropic's lead when they inevitably build similar systems. Because if history teaches us anything about powerful technology, it's that keeping it contained rarely works forever.

For now, we're in the strange position of hoping that 40 tech companies can use an AI to secure our digital infrastructure faster than bad actors can figure out how to exploit the same vulnerabilities. It's not exactly a comforting thought, but it might be the best option we have in an increasingly AI-powered world.
