When an AI company says their latest model is too dangerous for public release, you know we're entering uncharted territory. That's exactly what happened this week when Anthropic dropped a bombshell about their new Claude Mythos Preview model — an AI so skilled at finding security vulnerabilities that they're keeping it locked away from the general public.

![Anthropic's new model will be named Claude Mythos Preview](https://static01.nyt.com/images/2026/04/06/business/ROOSE-claude/ROOSE-claude-videoSixteenByNine3000.jpg)

Instead of the usual fanfare and public launch, Anthropic is taking a radically different approach. They're sharing Claude Mythos with a select group of over 40 major tech companies — including Apple, Amazon, Microsoft, Google, Cisco, and Broadcom — through an initiative called Project Glasswing. The goal? Use this AI's scary-good vulnerability detection skills to actually strengthen our digital defenses before the bad guys get their hands on similar technology.

## The Cybersecurity 'Reckoning' Is Here

This isn't just corporate caution — it's a wake-up call. Logan Graham, who leads Anthropic's team responsible for testing new models for dangerous capabilities, didn't mince words when describing what Claude Mythos represents. He called it "the starting point for what we think will be an industry change point, or reckoning, with what needs to happen now."

The implications are massive. If an AI can systematically identify security flaws in software with unprecedented accuracy, imagine what happens when cybercriminals get access to similar capabilities. We're talking about a potential arms race where the weapons are lines of code that can expose vulnerabilities faster than human security experts can patch them.

## A $100 Million Bet on Good Guys Winning

Anthropic isn't just talking the talk — they're putting serious money behind this initiative. The company is committing up to $100 million in Claude usage credits to Project Glasswing, ensuring that the consortium members can thoroughly test and strengthen their systems.

"The goal is both to raise awareness and to give good actors a head start on the process of securing open-source and private infrastructure and code," explained Jared Kaplan, Anthropic's chief science officer.

This approach makes strategic sense. By working with organizations like the Linux Foundation, which maintains critical open-source software that powers much of the internet, Anthropic is trying to shore up the digital infrastructure we all depend on.

## What This Means for the Rest of Us

While most of us won't get to play with Claude Mythos directly, its existence signals a new phase in AI development where companies are grappling with the dual-use nature of their technology. The same AI that could revolutionize cybersecurity defense could also supercharge cyberattacks if it falls into the wrong hands.

This controlled release strategy might become the new playbook for AI companies developing particularly powerful models. Rather than the traditional "move fast and break things" approach, we're seeing a more cautious "move carefully and fix things" mentality.

## The Bigger Picture

Claude Mythos Preview represents more than just another AI milestone — it's a glimpse into a future where the most powerful AI models might not be available to everyone. This could mark the beginning of a two-tiered AI ecosystem: powerful, restricted models for critical infrastructure and security work, and more accessible (but less capable) models for general use.

The success of Project Glasswing could determine whether this approach becomes the industry standard or just an interesting experiment. Either way, one thing is clear: the age of AI models being too powerful for public consumption has officially begun.

As we navigate this new landscape, the key question isn't just what AI can do — it's what we should allow it to do, and who gets to make that call. Anthropic's decision to prioritize security over openness might just be the responsible choice we needed, even if it means some of the coolest tech stays behind closed doors.
