The narrative that young people are AI's biggest cheerleaders just got complicated. While over half of Gen Z Americans are actively using generative AI tools, their enthusiasm is taking a nosedive — and that should worry everyone in tech.

## The Numbers Tell a Story of Growing Skepticism

A new study from Gallup, the Walton Family Foundation, and education tech venture firm GSV Ventures surveyed more than 1,500 people aged 14-29 between February and March 2026. The results paint a picture of a generation that's using AI out of necessity, not excitement.

The most striking finding? Hope about AI plummeted from 27% to just 18% in a single year. Meanwhile, nearly one-third of respondents said AI made them feel angry — a sentiment that's becoming harder to ignore as these tools become workplace staples.

"In most of these cases, Gen Z-ers have become increasingly skeptical, increasingly negative — from a place where even last year, they weren't particularly positive about it," explained Zach Hrynowski, Gallup's senior education researcher who led the study.

## Workplace Fears Drive the Backlash

The workplace anxiety is real and measurable. Among young adults already in the workforce, nearly half believe AI's risks outweigh its benefits — an 11-point jump from last year. Only 15% see AI as a net positive for their careers.

This isn't just about job displacement fears, though that's certainly part of it. Gen Z workers are worried about something more fundamental: losing their ability to think creatively and critically. They're concerned that relying on AI tools might atrophy skills they haven't even fully developed yet.

## The Efficiency Paradox

Here's where it gets interesting: these same young adults acknowledge that AI makes them more efficient at school and work. They're using ChatGPT for research, Claude for writing assistance, and various AI tools for productivity gains. But efficiency isn't everything when you're trying to build a career.

"They see the immediate benefits, but they're thinking long-term about what this means for their development as professionals and thinkers," Hrynowski noted. It's a surprisingly mature perspective from a generation often dismissed as tech-obsessed.

## What This Means for the AI Industry

This attitude shift should be a wake-up call for AI companies banking on young users as their core audience. Gen Z isn't rejecting AI wholesale — they're becoming more discerning about how and when they use it.

The growing anger suggests deeper issues around trust, transparency, and control. Unlike older generations who might avoid AI tools entirely, Gen Z is living with them daily and experiencing the friction firsthand.

## The Bigger Picture

This survey reveals something crucial about AI adoption: usage doesn't equal enthusiasm. Gen Z might be the most AI-literate generation, but they're also becoming its most thoughtful critics.

As these young adults enter the workforce and gain economic power, their skepticism could shape how AI develops. Companies that ignore this growing unease do so at their own risk.

The takeaway isn't that AI is doomed — it's that the honeymoon phase is over, and now comes the harder work of building technology that people actually want to live with long-term.
