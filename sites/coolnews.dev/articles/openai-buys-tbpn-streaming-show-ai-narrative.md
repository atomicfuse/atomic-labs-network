OpenAI just made a move that's equal parts smart and telling — they bought "TBPN," one of tech's hottest streaming shows. The ChatGPT maker announced Thursday it acquired the popular tech talk show for an undisclosed amount, essentially buying themselves a direct pipeline to tech-savvy audiences.

Here's the thing: this isn't just about expanding their media portfolio. It's damage control with a marketing twist.

## Why OpenAI Needed a PR Makeover

For the past 18 months, "TBPN" hosts John Coogan and Jordi Hays have been the golden boys of tech media. Their three-hour daily show has become Silicon Valley's unofficial town hall, with every major CEO scrambling to get on their couch. The show airs online five days a week and has built a devoted following among tech enthusiasts who appreciate their generally optimistic take on emerging technologies.

But here's what's really driving this acquisition: public sentiment around AI is shifting fast, and not in OpenAI's favor. What started as curiosity about AI capabilities has morphed into genuine concern about AI's potential to disrupt everything from jobs to the environment. Even Bernie Sanders has joined the chorus of critics raising alarms about AI's societal impact.

![Mike Isaac headshot](https://static01.nyt.com/images/2018/02/16/multimedia/author-mike-isaac/author-mike-isaac-thumbLarge-v3.png)

## The Marketing Strategy Behind the Deal

Fidji Simo, a top OpenAI executive who championed this acquisition, was refreshingly honest about their motivations. She praised TBPN's "marketing instincts" and wants to leverage the hosts' influence "outside of the show" to get people more excited about OpenAI's tech.

In an internal memo, Simo admitted that "the standard communications playbook just doesn't apply to us." Translation: traditional PR isn't working when you're reshaping society with artificial intelligence.

The goal? Creating what Simo calls "a space for a real, constructive conversation about the changes A.I. creates — with builders and people using the technology at the center."

## What Changes for TBPN

Despite the acquisition, TBPN claims it will maintain editorial independence. The 11-person team will continue their daily show format but will shut down their advertising operations. They'll now report to Chris Lehane, OpenAI's chief global affairs officer, who oversees communications and policy.

This setup raises obvious questions about true editorial independence when your paycheck comes from the company you're covering. But OpenAI seems willing to bet that TBPN's authentic voice is worth more than heavy-handed control.

## The Bigger Picture

This acquisition reflects a broader challenge facing AI companies in 2026: how do you maintain public support when your technology is fundamentally changing how society works? OpenAI clearly believes that authentic, enthusiastic voices like TBPN's hosts are more valuable than traditional advertising or corporate communications.

Whether this strategy works depends on TBPN's ability to maintain credibility while being owned by one of the most scrutinized companies in tech. For OpenAI, it's a calculated gamble that the right messengers can shift the narrative from AI fear to AI excitement.

The real test will be whether audiences continue to trust TBPN's take on AI developments when they know who's signing the checks.
