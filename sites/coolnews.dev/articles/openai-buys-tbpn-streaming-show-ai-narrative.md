In a move that screams "we need better PR," OpenAI just dropped an undisclosed amount of cash to buy "TBPN," a tech-focused streaming show that's become Silicon Valley's favorite podcast for the past 18 months. The deal is OpenAI's latest attempt to control the narrative around AI as public opinion shifts from "wow, cool tech" to "wait, this might actually be terrifying."

## The Show Every Tech CEO Wants to Be On

For context, "TBPN" hosts John Coogan and Jordi Hays have built something special — a daily streaming show that runs three hours a day, five days a week, covering technology and business with an optimistic spin that tech executives absolutely love. Every major CEO in Silicon Valley has been practically begging to get on their show, which tells you everything about how influential this platform has become.

The acquisition puts "TBPN" under OpenAI's strategy division, where it'll report to Chris Lehane, the company's chief global affairs officer who handles communications and policy. Translation: this is 100% about messaging and damage control.

## Why OpenAI Really Bought a Streaming Show

Let's be real — this isn't about supporting independent media. OpenAI is facing a PR nightmare as criticism of AI technology intensifies from every direction. Environmental groups are calling out the massive energy consumption, labor advocates like Bernie Sanders are sounding alarms about job displacement, and the general public is getting increasingly spooked by AI's rapid development.

![Mike Isaac, technology reporter covering AI developments](https://static01.nyt.com/images/2018/02/16/multimedia/author-mike-isaac/author-mike-isaac-thumbLarge-v3.png)

Fidji Simo, the OpenAI executive who championed this deal, was refreshingly honest about the company's motivations. She praised the show's "marketing instincts" and wants to use the hosts' talents "outside of the show" to make people more excited about OpenAI's technology. In an internal memo, she admitted that "the standard communications playbook just doesn't apply to us."

## Editorial Independence (With a Huge Asterisk)

"TBPN" claims it will maintain editorial independence while winding down its advertising operations. The 11-person team will continue their daily show format, but now they're essentially on OpenAI's payroll. It's hard to imagine how that doesn't influence coverage, especially when your new boss is actively looking to reshape public perception of AI.

Simo said the goal is to "create a space for a real, constructive conversation about the changes A.I. creates — with builders and people using the technology at the center." That's corporate speak for "we want to tell our side of the story without all the inconvenient criticism."

## The Bigger Picture: AI Companies Fighting for Hearts and Minds

This acquisition represents a broader trend of AI companies desperately trying to get ahead of negative narratives. As AI capabilities rapidly advance, public skepticism is turning into genuine concern about the technology's impact on jobs, privacy, and society as a whole.

OpenAI's decision to buy a streaming platform rather than just sponsor content shows how seriously they're taking the narrative war. By owning the platform, they gain much more control over messaging and can ensure their perspective gets prominent placement in tech media discourse.

## What This Means for Tech Media

The "TBPN" acquisition raises important questions about media independence in the tech space. When AI companies start buying media properties to influence coverage, it becomes harder for audiences to trust what they're hearing. Even with promises of editorial independence, the financial relationship fundamentally changes the dynamic.

For viewers of "TBPN," the key will be watching how coverage of OpenAI and AI criticism evolves over time. If the show becomes noticeably softer on AI risks or more promotional of OpenAI's products, that editorial independence claim won't hold much water.

The bottom line: OpenAI is betting that owning a popular tech streaming show will help them win the narrative battle around AI development. Whether audiences will notice the difference — or care — remains to be seen.
