## The Scale of Resource Extraction

A new study from ICTA-UAB has quantified the massive scale of resource extraction from Latin America through unequal trade relationships with wealthier nations. According to the research, in 2020 alone, the Global North extracted 935 million tonnes of materials, utilized 4 million square kilometers of land, and benefited from 53 billion hours of labor from Latin American countries.

These figures represent the hidden infrastructure supporting consumption patterns in developed economies, revealing how everyday products consumed in wealthier nations carry significant ecological and labor costs that remain largely invisible to end consumers.

## Understanding Ecologically Unequal Exchange

The research framework centers on what economists call "ecologically unequal exchange" - a system where developing nations export raw materials and labor-intensive products while importing higher-value manufactured goods. According to the study, this trade pattern reinforces Latin America's peripheral role in the global economy.

This system creates a dependency cycle where Latin American countries remain suppliers of primary commodities rather than developing their own value-added industries. The extracted resources support high consumption levels in richer countries while the environmental and social costs remain concentrated in the exporting regions.

The study's data reveals the extent to which Global North consumption depends on Latin American inputs, creating potential supply chain risks for multinational corporations and investors. With 4 million square kilometers of land supporting export production, any disruption to agricultural or mining operations could have significant downstream effects.

Climate change, political instability, or shifts in trade policy could disrupt these resource flows, potentially affecting everything from food prices to manufacturing inputs. Companies with significant exposure to Latin American supply chains may need to reassess their risk management strategies.
