There's no shortage of cooking advice out there, but not all of it is accurate. Let's debunk seven common myths about healthy cooking that may be compromising your attempts at a nutritious lifestyle.

## Myth 1: 'Cooking Vegetables Depletes Their Nutrients'

While it's true that some nutrients can be lost in the cooking process, it doesn't mean that cooked vegetables are devoid of nutritional value. In fact, some nutrients, like lycopene in tomatoes, become more bioavailable when cooked.

## Myth 2: 'Microwaving Zaps Nutrients'

Contrary to popular belief, microwaving doesn't rob your food of nutrients. The loss of nutrients in food is more related to the cooking time, temperature, and amount of water used, rather than the cooking method itself. Microwaving, in fact, might even help to preserve certain nutrients due to its short cooking times.

## Myth 3: 'Frozen Fruits and Vegetables Aren't as Nutritious as Fresh'

Don't dismiss frozen fruits and vegetables as less nutritious. They are often picked at their peak and flash-frozen, which can lock in their nutrients. In some cases, they might even be more nutritious than fresh options that have been sitting on store shelves for a while.

## Myth 4: 'All Fats Are Bad'

The notion that all fats are detrimental to your health is simply untrue. While it's advisable to limit your intake of saturated and trans fats, unsaturated fats, like those found in avocados, nuts, and olive oil, are beneficial for your heart health.

## Myth 5: 'Eggs Raise Cholesterol Levels'

Eggs have often been vilified due to their cholesterol content, but recent research has shown that dietary cholesterol doesn't have the same impact on blood cholesterol as was once thought. In moderation, eggs can certainly be part of a healthy diet.

## Myth 6: 'Gluten-Free Means Healthier'

Going gluten-free isn't inherently healthier unless you have a condition like celiac disease. Gluten-free products can often be lower in fiber and higher in sugar and fat than their gluten-containing counterparts.

## Myth 7: 'Sea Salt Has Less Sodium Than Table Salt'

By weight, both sea salt and table salt contain about the same amount of sodium. The difference lies in their taste, texture, and processing, not their sodium content.

To conclude, it's essential to make informed decisions about your diet based on accurate information. Don't let these myths steer you away from a balanced, nutritious lifestyle. Stay informed, stay healthy.
