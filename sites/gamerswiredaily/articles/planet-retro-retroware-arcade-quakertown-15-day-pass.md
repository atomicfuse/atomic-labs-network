Planet Retro Video Games in Quakertown is opening Retroware Arcade next week, and the pitch is straightforward: pay $15 at the door, stay as long as you want, and work through 30-plus arcade games without feeding quarters into anything. By the end of this piece you'll know exactly what's waiting inside, who it's built for, and whether it's worth the trip.

## What Retroware Arcade Actually Is

This isn't a bolt-on room with a couple of dusty cabinets. Retroware Arcade is a deliberate expansion of Planet Retro Video Games, the existing Quakertown shop, into a full standalone arcade experience. The owner has described it as a retro-meets-modern setup, which in practice means you'll find beloved classics sitting alongside Japanese rhythm games — a wider range than the name alone might suggest.

The all-day admission model is the headline feature. At $15, you're not rationing your time or your tokens. You can spend forty-five minutes on one machine, walk away, grab a drink, and come back without losing anything. For families especially, that removes the single most annoying part of a traditional arcade: watching your kid's eyes glaze over as the credits run out mid-game.

## The Game Lineup: Classics and a Few Surprises

Galaga and Jr. Pac-Man are the anchor names the store itself is highlighting — and for good reason. Both have the kind of immediate pick-up-and-play feel that works whether you last touched a joystick in 1985 or never have. Galaga in particular rewards patience and pattern recognition in a way that holds up embarrassingly well.

Beyond those marquee titles, the 30-plus game count means there's real depth here. The retro-meets-modern framing suggests the lineup doesn't stop at the golden age of arcades — the inclusion of Japanese rhythm games points to a curated selection that should give regulars something to master over multiple visits, not just a nostalgia loop that gets old after an hour.

That mix matters. A room full of only '80s shooters would bring in Gen X for a weekend and then go quiet. Rhythm games attract a completely different crowd and skill set, which helps justify the all-day format — there's actually enough variety to fill the time.

## Who This Is Built For

The owner has been direct about the local context: Quakertown doesn't have a lot of interactive entertainment options. Retroware Arcade is explicitly positioned as an all-ages destination, and the design backs that up.

**Families** get a clean, fixed-cost outing. $15 per person is a reasonable ceiling for an afternoon, and the all-day pass means you're not rushing anyone.

**Gen X gamers** get the nostalgia hit done right — not a themed bar with two machines, but a proper arcade with the games they actually remember.

**Younger players** get access to titles, including rhythm games, that they may know from their phones or consoles but have never played on dedicated hardware. That's a genuinely different experience.

The store has also flagged party options, which positions Retroware Arcade as a birthday venue — a practical angle that makes the business model stickier than walk-in traffic alone.

## Expanded Hours and Planning Your Visit

Alongside the arcade launch, Planet Retro is expanding its hours, which suggests the store is betting on this becoming a regular destination rather than a one-time curiosity. If you're planning a visit, the opening is set for next week, so the first weekend will likely bring the biggest crowds — worth keeping in mind if you want more breathing room at the machines.

The $15 all-day pass structure means your best move is to show up without a strict schedule. Block out two to three hours if you want to genuinely work through the lineup. Rhythm games especially tend to eat time in the best possible way once you start chasing high scores.

If you're scouting it as a party venue, the combination of a fixed admission price and a dedicated arcade space makes budgeting straightforward — you know the per-head cost upfront, which is more than you can say for most entertainment venues.

## Why the $15 Model Changes the Feel of the Place

There's a reason the all-day flat rate is worth talking about separately. Traditional token or card-based arcades create a constant low-level pressure: every game costs something, and that cost shapes how you play and how long you stay. You ration your attempts. You leave when the credits run out, not when you're actually done.

Flat admission removes that. You're suddenly free to fail repeatedly at Galaga's stage five, not because you're grinding for a high score but because it's genuinely fun to try. That shift in psychology — from transaction to experience — is what turns an arcade into a hangout rather than a quick stop.

For a town the owner describes as underserved for interactive entertainment, that's not a small thing. A place where you can spend a few hours without watching a meter tick down is, almost by definition, a third place — somewhere between home and a destination with a cover charge. Those spaces are harder to find than they should be.

## Worth the Trip?

If you're within a reasonable drive of Quakertown and you have any fondness for arcade gaming — whether that's Galaga-era classics or modern rhythm titles — Retroware Arcade's $15 all-day format is about as low a barrier as you'll find for a full afternoon out. Go in the first week if you want to be part of the opening energy, or wait for the crowds to settle if you'd rather have the machines to yourself. Either way, the lineup is deep enough that one visit probably won't be the last.
