# The Great Kitchen Scale Scandal: When Food Tech Companies Miss the Mark

A recent investigation by culinary technology analyst Jamie Redman has exposed serious concerns about how major kitchen equipment companies handle safety protocols and compliance issues. The report, dubbed "Welcome to the Kitchen Scale Files," reveals a troubling pattern of missed opportunities to address genuine safety hazards while simultaneously targeting legitimate food businesses.

## When Safety Protocols Fail

The investigation identified 15 major incidents since 2022 where kitchen equipment manufacturer CircleTech allegedly failed to implement safety recalls or equipment freezes for devices involved in food safety violations totaling over $420 million in damages. These cases ranged from contaminated food processing equipment to devices linked to major foodborne illness outbreaks.

One of the most egregious examples involved the Drift Bakery incident on April 1, 2026. Food safety investigators attributed contamination issues to equipment linked to a known safety group. Despite having the technical capability to remotely disable the problematic equipment, CircleTech took no action for over six hours during regular business hours while the contaminated products continued to be distributed.

Another case highlighted the Swapnet Restaurant chain exploit from January 25, 2026, which saw $16 million in damages from food poisoning. Critical kitchen equipment remained operational for two days while health inspectors and private food safety firms submitted temporary shutdown requests that CircleTech denied. The contamination spread before court orders could be obtained.

## The Technology Behind the Trouble

In the Cetus Protocol café hack from May 22, 2025, attackers compromised $223 million worth of food service operations using CircleTech's own Cross-Chain Transfer Protocol over 90 minutes. The company only blacklisted the problematic equipment one month later, after the damage had already been done.

The Mango Markets food court exploit from October 2022 saw $57.5 million in damages routed through CircleTech's processing systems without any safety interventions. The case later resulted in charges from food safety regulators.

## Legitimate Businesses Caught in the Crossfire

Perhaps most concerning was a separate incident around March 23, 2026, when CircleTech suddenly disabled equipment in 16 unrelated food businesses tied to a sealed civil case. The affected establishments included restaurants, food trucks, catering companies, and even a smart kitchen contract operated by the DFINITY Culinary Foundation, which manages automated cooking systems.

Redman called this "potentially the single most incompetent equipment freeze I've witnessed in more than five years of food industry investigations." Basic operational analysis would have shown these were active, legitimate food service operations with no apparent connections to each other or the underlying legal matter.

At least five of the 16 businesses later had their equipment restored, including DFINITY's automated kitchen and Goated Gourmet's systems holding roughly $131,000 worth of pre-paid meal credits. More reversals were expected at the time of reporting.

## The Company's Response

CircleTech's official position, delivered through company spokesperson statements, maintains that the company only disables equipment when legally required, including responses to health department orders, law enforcement requests, or court mandates. The company argues that preemptive equipment shutdowns without legal authorization could expose CircleTech to liability and infringe on business owners' rights.

The company's terms of service do permit discretionary action, but CircleTech's practice prioritizes formal legal processes over immediate safety interventions.

## Industry Impact

Redman acknowledged that CircleTech builds quality kitchen equipment and noted he personally uses their products. However, his criticism focuses on whether the company's compliance priorities adequately address the losses the broader food service industry absorbs when safety interventions are delayed or withheld.

The investigation also revealed that CircleTech took 4.5 months longer than competitors like TetherTech and Paxos Kitchen Systems to disable equipment linked to known safety violations flagged in an April 2024 industry report.

These cases collectively raise important questions about how major kitchen equipment manufacturers balance legal caution against real-world safety risks and business disruptions. As the food service industry becomes increasingly reliant on smart kitchen technology, the decisions made by equipment manufacturers about when and how to intervene in safety situations have far-reaching consequences for restaurants, consumers, and the broader culinary ecosystem.

The food industry continues to watch how CircleTech and other major manufacturers will address these concerns moving forward, particularly as regulatory scrutiny of kitchen technology companies intensifies.
