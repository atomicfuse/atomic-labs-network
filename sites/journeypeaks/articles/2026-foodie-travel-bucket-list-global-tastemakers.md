Food & Wine just released its 2026 Global Tastemakers Awards, and if you've ever wanted a shortlist of where to eat, sleep, fly, and sail — curated by people who actually know — this is it. Now in its fourth year, the awards pull votes from more than 400 chefs, travel experts, and food and travel writers worldwide, then run the results through a Global Advisory Board before anything gets called a winner. The result is one of the more credible maps available for planning a food-first trip.

By the end of this piece, you'll know how to use the award categories as a practical framework — not just a reading list — to sketch out a real itinerary around the world's best culinary experiences in 2026.

## Start With the Category That Drives Your Trip

The Global Tastemakers covers six distinct categories: restaurants, bars, cruises, hotels, airlines, and airports. Most food-travel guides stop at restaurants. This one doesn't, and that's exactly what makes it useful for planning.

Before you open a map, figure out which category anchors your trip. Are you chasing a specific dining experience — a restaurant that's been on your list for years? Or is the journey itself the point, in which case a cruise or airline ranking is your entry point? The category you care most about should shape the geography, not the other way around.

**If a restaurant is the anchor:** Let the winning restaurant determine your destination city, then build outward — find a hotel on the list in the same city, identify the best bar nearby, and check whether a top-ranked airline flies that route.

**If a hotel is the anchor:** Luxury food-focused hotels often have award-winning restaurants on property. A winning hotel is rarely just a place to sleep — it's frequently a culinary destination in its own right.

**If a cruise is the anchor:** Culinary cruises are a category unto themselves. A top-ranked cruise gives you multiple ports, multiple cuisines, and the ship's own dining program — which, at this level, can rival land-based restaurants.

## Use the Awards to Compare Destinations, Not Just Venues

The real power of a multi-category award is cross-referencing. When a single city shows up across restaurant, bar, and hotel categories, that's a signal — not a coincidence. It means the local food culture is deep enough to sustain world-class experiences across different types of venues.

As you read through the 2026 winners, make a simple tally: which cities appear in more than one category? Those are your strongest candidates for a food-led trip, because you're not betting everything on a single reservation. If the restaurant is fully booked, you still have a world-class bar around the corner and a hotel dining room worth the visit.

This approach also surfaces emerging destinations — cities that might not be on your radar yet but are clearly building culinary momentum if they're showing up in the rankings for the first time.

## Don't Overlook Airlines and Airports

This is the category most food travelers skip, and it's a mistake. A top-ranked airline for culinary experience can meaningfully change a long-haul trip — particularly in business or first class, where the food program is often developed in partnership with notable chefs.

Similarly, a highly ranked airport tells you something practical: if you have a layover there, you can eat well without defaulting to an airport chain. Some of the world's best airport food is legitimately good by any standard, and knowing which terminals deliver before you fly saves you from a forgettable meal between connections.

When you're booking flights for a food trip, run a quick check: does your routing take you through any of the award-winning airports? If you have flexibility on connections, a layover in the right terminal is a free bonus meal.

## Turn One Award Winner Into a Multi-Day Itinerary

Here's a concrete method for building a trip around the rankings:

1. **Pick your anchor winner** — one restaurant, hotel, or cruise that genuinely excites you.
2. **Identify the destination** — city, region, or port of call.
3. **Cross-reference other categories** for winners in the same location.
4. **Map the gaps** — what's missing from the awards list that the destination is known for? Local markets, street food, neighborhood spots the awards don't cover but travel writers mention? Add those to balance the high-end with the everyday.
5. **Check the logistics** — does a top-ranked airline serve this route? Does the airport rating matter for your connection?
6. **Book in order of difficulty** — the most in-demand restaurant first, then hotel, then flights.

This sequence keeps you from planning a beautiful trip around a reservation you can't get.

## A Note on Using Awards as a Starting Point, Not a Final Word

The Global Tastemakers rankings are voted on by over 400 industry professionals and shaped by a Global Advisory Board — that's meaningful signal. But awards reflect a moment in time, and a restaurant that wins in 2026 may have changed chefs or direction by the time you visit. Always cross-check with recent reviews closer to your travel dates.

The awards are also weighted toward the high end of the market by nature. Use them to identify destination cities and anchor experiences, then fill the rest of your trip with local recommendations — the neighborhood spot a winning chef actually eats at on their day off is often the meal you remember longest.

## The Practical Takeaway

The 2026 Food & Wine Global Tastemakers list is most useful when you treat it as a framework, not a finished itinerary. Pick the category that matters most to you, find where multiple winners overlap geographically, and build your trip outward from there. Book the hardest reservation first, let the destination surprise you everywhere else, and check the airport rankings before your next long connection — there might be a genuinely good meal waiting between flights.
