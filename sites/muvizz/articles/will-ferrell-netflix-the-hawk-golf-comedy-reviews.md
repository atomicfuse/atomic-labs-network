## The Setup: Ferrell, Golf, and High Expectations

On paper, *The Hawk* sounds like a reasonable bet. Will Ferrell doing broad sports comedy is basically a proven format — the man helped make *Blades of Glory* and *Talladega Nights* into cult touchstones, and the golf genre has its own beloved absurdist landmark in *Happy Gilmore*. Slate's review specifically name-drops *Happy Gilmore* and *Stick* in its framing, which signals that critics came in knowing exactly what the show was reaching for and felt it fell short of its own reference points.

That's a tougher verdict than simply being bad in a vacuum. Being bad while chasing a beloved template is the kind of miss that tends to sting, because the audience can see both what you were going for and where you landed.

## "A Stellar Supporting Cast" — Wasted

The Hollywood Reporter's review adds a wrinkle that's worth sitting with: it's not just Ferrell the critics are lamenting, it's the ensemble around him. Per THR, the supporting cast is described as "stellar" — and yet, per the same review, they're wasted by the material. Netflix's own cast guide breaks down the show's characters as Lonnie (presumably Ferrell's character), his family, his friends, and his foes, suggesting the show is built around a fairly traditional ensemble structure.

A wasted strong supporting cast is its own specific flavor of disappointment. It implies the problem isn't talent on screen but something upstream — writing, premise, execution — which is the harder kind of problem to wave away.

## Should You Actually Watch It?

Here's where the honest answer gets a little complicated. Critics and audiences don't always align, especially on broad comedy, and especially on Netflix, where the threshold for "worth putting on" is lower than a theatrical commitment. Ferrell has a specific fanbase that will show up for his energy regardless of review scores, and a golf-comedy premise with a stacked ensemble might land differently on a Friday night couch than it does under a critic's analytical eye.

That said, both Slate and THR are calling this a miss — and these aren't contrarian takes designed to be provocative. They're converging reviews from outlets that cover this genre seriously. When two credible voices land in the same place independently, that's signal worth factoring in.

The show's own Netflix cast guide is live if you want to get a feel for the characters before committing.
