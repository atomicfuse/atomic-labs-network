## The Right Tool for the Right Emission

A new study published in Nature suggests that temporary carbon dioxide removal could play a valuable role in achieving climate goals—but only when used correctly. According to the research, temporary carbon storage should be used to offset short-lived pollutants like methane, rather than long-lived CO2 emissions from fossil fuels.

The findings come at a critical time when carbon offsets are facing increasing scrutiny from policymakers and environmental advocates. Many question whether current offset programs truly deliver the climate benefits they promise, particularly when temporary carbon storage is used to justify continued fossil fuel emissions.

## A Physics-Based Framework for Carbon Accounting

The Nature study proposes a physics-based accounting framework that matches the duration of carbon storage to the climate impact of different greenhouse gases. This approach recognizes a fundamental difference between gases: while CO2 remains in the atmosphere for centuries, methane breaks down much faster but has a more intense warming effect in the short term.

According to reports, the research provides example ratios for matching temporary storage duration to methane's climate impact. This mathematical approach offers a more scientifically grounded way to evaluate the effectiveness of different carbon removal strategies.

## Why Storage Duration Matters

The study emphasizes that the quality of carbon removal depends heavily on three key factors: storage time, gas type, and accounting rules. A 20-year carbon storage project has vastly different climate implications than a 100-year storage initiative, particularly when considering what type of emissions it's meant to offset.

For methane emissions, which naturally break down in the atmosphere over approximately 20 years, temporary carbon storage of similar duration could provide genuine climate benefits. However, using the same temporary storage to offset CO2 emissions—which persist for hundreds of years—would significantly overstate the climate value.

## Implications for Agriculture and Hard-to-Abate Sectors

The research offers particular relevance for agriculture and other hard-to-abate sectors that produce persistent methane emissions. Livestock farming, rice cultivation, and certain industrial processes continue to generate methane even as other sectors transition to cleaner alternatives.

According to the study, temporary carbon removal could provide these sectors with a more credible pathway to reduce their climate impact. Rather than treating all emissions equally, this approach acknowledges the different characteristics of various greenhouse gases and matches removal strategies accordingly.

Livestock-heavy countries, in particular, could benefit from this framework as they seek to balance agricultural production with climate commitments.

## The Case for Two-Basket Climate Accounting

The research supports what some experts call "two-basket" climate accounting—treating short-lived and long-lived greenhouse gases as separate categories requiring different offset strategies. This approach could help prevent the misuse of temporary carbon removal as a substitute for deep decarbonization in fossil fuel-dependent sectors.

Under this framework, companies and governments would need to address CO2 emissions primarily through permanent carbon removal or emissions reductions, while using temporary storage specifically for methane and other short-lived pollutants.

## Addressing Offset Credibility Concerns

The timing of this research coincides with growing debates over carbon offset credibility. Many current offset programs have faced criticism for overstating their climate benefits or allowing companies to continue high-emission practices while claiming carbon neutrality.

By providing clear guidelines for when and how temporary carbon removal should be used, the study aims to give policymakers and companies a more scientifically robust approach to carbon accounting. This could help restore confidence in offset programs while ensuring they deliver genuine climate benefits.

## Looking Forward

The research suggests that temporary carbon removal isn't a free pass for continued fossil fuel emissions, but rather a targeted tool for addressing specific types of pollution. As governments and companies refine their net-zero strategies, this physics-based approach could help ensure that carbon removal efforts are both effective and credible.

The study's framework may influence future policy decisions about carbon offset standards and could reshape how different sectors approach their climate commitments. For the agricultural sector and other methane-producing industries, it offers a pathway to meaningful climate action without compromising their essential functions.
