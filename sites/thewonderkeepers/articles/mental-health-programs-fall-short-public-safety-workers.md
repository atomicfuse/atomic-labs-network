Mental health programs are often seen as a panacea for the emotional toll endured by public safety workers, yet recent findings reveal a critical gap in this approach. A Ontario Coroner's Review detailed a troubling statistic: there were 34 deaths by suicide among correctional workers over the past 15 years, with 17 of these tragedies occurring since 2020. This stark reality urges a re-examination of how we address psychological injury in high-stress occupations.

## The Troubling Trend in Public Safety Work

In recent years, public safety workers such as correctional staff, dispatchers, firefighters, paramedics, and police have faced an alarming rise in psychological injuries. Between 2014 and 2018 alone, Ontario witnessed a staggering 400% increase in compensation claims linked to mental health issues in these sectors. This surge demonstrates that while organizations may be investing in mental health programs, these initiatives alone do not stem the tide of psychological harm.

### The Hidden Costs of Psychological Injury

The impacts of psychological injury extend beyond the individual. Employers bear significant costs associated with rising compensation claims, impacting not only their workforce but also their financial health. When public safety workers struggle with psychological injury, the consequences reverberate through their families, communities, and ultimately, the public they serve. The picture painted is one of urgent need for systemic change in the workplace environment to address these growing concerns.

## Why Mental Health Programs Aren't Enough

More often than not, organizations default to implementing wellness programs, resilience training, or therapy apps when addressing mental health. While these tools can be helpful, they fall short if the underlying issues within the organization remain unaddressed. Significant factors impacting mental health include:

1. **Understaffing**: Overworked employees face extreme stress, leading to burnout and mental health crises.
2. **Poor Leadership**: Lacking support and clear guidance can exacerbate feelings of isolation and despair.
3. **Harassment**: A toxic work culture can lead to significant emotional harm, making it hard for individuals to seek help.
4. **Inadequate Resources**: Without proper training, support systems, and staffing levels, workers face insurmountable challenges that contribute to mental strain.

A focus solely on personal resilience is tunes out the pressing need for transformative changes that can enhance workplace culture and conditions.

## Shifting the Focus: From Individual Resilience to Systemic Solutions

The argument for shifting focus is simple: we must prioritize creating a healthier environment before expecting wellness programs to make a significant impact. Public safety organizations can start to bridge this gap by:

- Assessing and improving the work culture to foster open dialogue about mental health and well-being.
- Providing proper staffing levels to reduce the pressure on existing personnel.
- Implementing leadership training focused on supporting staff mental health and engagement.
- Establishing comprehensive resources that address the unique challenges faced by each discipline within public safety.

Confidential and flexible mental health care is essential for first responders who often navigate a culture of silence concerning their struggles. Support systems should encourage individuals to seek help without fear of repercussions.

## Conclusion: A Call to Action

As we aim to support the mental health of public safety workers, we must recognize the inadequacy of mental health programs when they exist within a framework of broken systems. A holistic approach—where improving workplace conditions is prioritized—holds the potential to foster true resilience among these dedicated individuals. The time to act is now, not just for the individuals burdened by these pressures, but for the communities that rely on their unwavering service. The challenge ahead is great, but so is the potential for meaningful change.
