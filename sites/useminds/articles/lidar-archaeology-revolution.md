In recent years, lidar technology has emerged as a transformative tool in archaeology, revealing ancient sites once thought lost to the lush canopies of forests and jungles. ## What Is Lidar and How Does It Work?
Lidar, which stands for Light Detection and Ranging, uses pulsed laser beams from an aircraft to scan the ground. These pulses bounce back to the sensor, allowing researchers to create a detailed 3D map of the landscape below. This reveals not only the surface features but also structures hidden beneath trees and underbrush, making it invaluable for archaeological explorations.

### Applications of Lidar in Archaeology
1. **Mapping Undocumented Sites**: According to Caverns, the use of lidar has uncovered numerous undocumented archaeological sites. His team's findings suggest that many ancient mound-builder cities in the U.S. are more extensive than previously realized.
2. **3D Reconstruction**: High-resolution drone scans are planned as part of the ongoing research to create 3D models of the identified mound-builder complexes. This technology allows not only visualization but a deeper understanding of the layout and structure of these ancient societies.
3. **Amazon Disruptions**: Lidar technology is poised to significantly enhance our understanding of the Amazon's historical landscape. Caverns noted a potential project aimed at conducting the largest lidar scan in the region, which could unveil colossal urban networks long concealed by dense vegetation.

## The Comparison: Mainstream vs. Independent Archaeology
The present day sees a fascinating divide between mainstream archaeology, often conducted by established institutions, and what some are calling 'outlaw archaeology.' 

- **Mainstream Archaeology**: Often reliant on extensive funding and institutional backing, this approach can be constrained by bureaucratic processes, slowing the pace of discovery.
- **Outlaw Archaeology**: Independent explorers like Caverns utilize publicly available lidar data alongside drones and AI tools to visualize lost worlds. This grassroots approach fosters a sense of community-driven discovery, pushing the boundaries of conventional archaeological methods.
